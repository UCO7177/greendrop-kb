Cooking Oil — What We Collect

What goes in the jug, what doesn't, and how to handle fats and solids.

We accept virtually all used cooking oils from household kitchens. If it cooked your food at some point, we want it back.

Accepted

  • Vegetable / canola oil
  • Sunflower oil
  • Olive oil (used)
  • Coconut oil (liquid form)
  • Peanut / sesame oil
  • Lard or bacon grease (melted)
  • Fish fry oil
  • Mixed frying oils

Not accepted

  • Motor oil or lubricants
  • Paint, solvents, chemicals
  • Expired sealed / unopened oil
  • Water or rinsed-pan liquid
  • Dairy-based sauces or milk
Quick rule: If it smells like food and was hot in a pan — it belongs in the jug, not the drain.

Contaminants can ruin entire collection batches, reduce the buyer value of the oil, and hurt your household's impact record. Here's what to avoid:

✗
Water or dishwater Even small amounts cause oil to separate and go rancid faster. Let the pan cool and pour off only the oil — don't rinse.
✗
Motor oil or petroleum products These are hazardous waste and require a completely separate collection stream. Never mix them with cooking oil.
✗
Household chemicals Bleach, cleaners, paint, solvents — none belong near the jug under any circumstances.
✗
Dairy or egg-heavy sauces Cream sauces and egg residues can spoil the batch. Pour off liquid oil only; scrape solids into the trash first.

You've got two weeks between pickups. Here's how to keep the jug safe, smell-free, and your kitchen happy.

1
Let oil cool completely (30–60 minutes)Hot oil can warp the HDPE jug and degrade the cap seal. Always wait before pouring.
2
Keep it tightly sealed between usesThe cap prevents odor, oxidation, and spill risk. Screw it on fully after every pour.
3
Store in a cool, dark spotUnder the sink, in a pantry, or the garage all work well. Avoid direct sunlight.
4
Don't overfillLeave 1–2 inches of headspace so oil doesn't expand and push past the cap seal.

Solid fats are absolutely accepted — they just need a moment of patience.

Rule of thumb: If you can pour it, we can take it. If it's solid at room temperature, let it warm to liquid first — or add it to liquid oil already in the jug and let it melt in.

Good approach

  • Let solidified fat warm to liquid
  • Mix into liquid oil in the jug
  • Collect in small jar, pour when liquid

Avoid

  • Scooping solid fat directly into jug
  • Pouring down the drain "just this once"
  • Throwing it in the trash (still a FOG risk)

Packing & Prep Guide

How to fill your jug correctly — from pan to pickup. No straining required on your end.

The whole routine takes under 90 seconds. Here it is from start to finish:

1
Cook your meal — enjoy itYou're already doing the hard part.
2
Let the pan cool (30–60 min)Don't rush. Hot oil is a burn risk and damages the jug's cap seal.
3
Pour slowly and sealFill to the shoulder of the jug (not the neck). Wipe the lip if oily, then screw the cap fully closed. No straining needed — we handle that at the consolidation point.
4
Set it out on pickup morningPlace by your front door or driveway edge by 7 AM on your assigned day. We'll swap it for a clean empty.

No — and that's intentional. We handle all straining at our consolidation point when we transfer collected oil into bulk containers. Your only job is to pour cooled oil into the jug and seal it.

Why we do it this way: Straining at home is the #1 reason people give up on recycling programs. Removing that step makes it as easy as possible for you — and a program everyone uses is far more valuable than a perfect process nobody follows.

You just do this

  • Let oil cool completely
  • Pour into the jug
  • Seal and store until pickup

Still avoid these

  • Adding water or rinsing the pan into the jug
  • Pouring in chemicals or non-food liquids
  • Overfilling past the shoulder of the jug

The labeled HDPE jug is yours to use for the program duration. Treat it like a reusable container:

✓
Keep the label visibleDon't peel or cover the GreenDrop label. It ties your household data to the right pickup record and tracks the jug through the swap cycle.
✓
Wipe the exterior if it gets oilyA paper towel does the job. A clean jug makes pickup easier and your neighbors appreciate it.
✗
Don't wash with soap and waterSoap residue and water both contaminate the oil. The jug doesn't need to be clean inside — just the right stuff in it.

Battery Recycling

Which batteries we accept, how to prepare them, and what to do with the ones we can't take.

We collect household batteries on the same pickup day as cooking oil — just place them in a small labeled bag next to your jug.

AA / AAARemotes, toys, devices
C / D cellsFlashlights, radios
9-voltSmoke detectors
Button / coin cellsWatches, key fobs
Rechargeable NiMHSmall packs, tools
Phone batteriesRemoved from device
Not sure? If it's smaller than your fist and powered a household device, it's almost certainly accepted. When in doubt, bag it and include it.

A little prep prevents hazards and keeps the collection safe for everyone on the route:

1
Tape the terminals on 9-volt batteriesThe two posts on a 9V can touch metal and cause a spark or fire in a bag. A small strip of electrical or masking tape across both terminals prevents this — this step is mandatory for 9Vs.
2
Place in a zip-lock or small paper bagDon't mix loose batteries with coins, keys, or foil. Contact between terminals generates heat. A small bag per type is ideal.
3
Label the bag "BATTERIES – GREENDROP"This helps us sort correctly at the consolidation point and keeps your impact stats accurate.
4
Set next to your oil jug on pickup daySame spot, same schedule. That's it.

Some battery types require specialized hazmat handling that's beyond our current program scope. For these, here are the best local alternatives:

Where to take them instead

  • Home Depot / Lowe's — lithium-ion drop boxes
  • Best Buy — in-store battery recycling
  • Miami-Dade household hazmat events
  • Call2Recycle drop points (call2recycle.org)

We cannot accept

  • Car / automotive batteries
  • Swollen or leaking batteries
  • Large lithium packs (e-bikes, power tools)
  • Industrial or commercial batteries

Batteries tossed in household trash end up in the South Dade Landfill — less than 2 miles from Black Point Marina. When casing degrades, heavy metals like lead, cadmium, and mercury leach into groundwater and eventually reach Biscayne Bay.

1
button cell battery can contaminate up to 1 square meter of soil for 50+ years when landfilled

By combining battery and oil recycling in one bi-weekly pickup, we remove the biggest barrier to responsible disposal: the need to make a special trip. It's already in your routine.

Why It Matters

The local environmental case — your pipes, Biscayne Bay, and the sewer system underneath us.

FOG — fats, oils, and grease — is the #1 cause of sewer blockages in Miami-Dade County. When poured down the drain, oil cools, congeals, and builds up on pipe walls over years, eventually causing backups and overflows.

1
It seems liquid — until it isn'tOil feels harmless when warm. As it cools in your drain pipe, it sticks to the walls and catches everything else flowing by.
2
Buildup accelerates over timeOne household's grease alone won't block a main line — but 50 households across two years absolutely can.
3
Overflows reach the bayWhen sewer mains back up, overflow points near South Dade discharge into wetlands and eventually Biscayne Bay.

Before the bay, before the sewer system — your own plumbing is the first thing at risk.

The honest math: Calling a plumber to clear a grease blockage typically costs $200–$500. The collection jug is free. This program literally pays you back.
🏠
Kitchen drain linesGrease accumulates fastest in the 20 feet of pipe between your sink and the main sewer line — and that section is almost always the homeowner's financial responsibility to maintain.
🏠
Septic systemsIf your home uses a septic system, grease is especially damaging — it forms a hardened scum mat that kills beneficial bacteria and can cause full system failure.

Our neighborhood sits at the intersection of several sensitive systems. What goes down our drains matters more here than almost anywhere else in the county.

🌊
Black Point MarinaLess than a mile from the neighborhood. Sewer overflows in South Dade have historically triggered fishing and swimming advisories at marina access points.
🏭
South Dade Wastewater Treatment PlantThe plant operates under a federal Consent Decree that legally requires Miami-Dade to reduce FOG loading in the sewer system. Our collected data contributes directly to that compliance record.
🗑️
South Dade LandfillEvery gallon of oil diverted is a gallon that doesn't enter landfill leachate. Cooking oil is organic, but in volume it displaces landfill capacity and creates runoff risk — especially this close to the bay.

Troubleshooting

Leaky jug? Wrong oil? Missed pickup? Here are the most common fixes.

?
My jug leaked in the cabinetCheck the cap seal — HDPE jugs can leak if the cap isn't fully threaded. Wipe the exterior, retighten the cap, store upright. If the jug is cracked, flag it at next pickup and we'll replace it at no cost.
?
My oil smells badUsed cooking oil does have an odor — this is normal. Storing it in a cabinet under the sink or pantry keeps smell contained. The sealed HDPE jug is designed to minimize this.
?
I missed my pickup dayNo action needed. Keep the full jug inside and set it out on the next cycle. If it's been more than 4 weeks without a pickup, reach out via the chatbot.
?
My neighbor wants to joinShare the chatbot link or this website. They can self-enroll if their address is in the service zone.

Don't panic. Here's what to do based on what got in:

💧
A small amount of water got inTablespoon or two — not a problem. The batch can absorb minor water content. Just don't rinse the jug intentionally going forward.
🥣
Food scraps fell inNot a problem — we strain all collected oil at the consolidation point anyway. Just seal the jug and set it out as normal.
⚠️
Something non-food or chemical went inDo not put this jug out for regular pickup. Message the chatbot immediately. We'll arrange a separate pickup and handle disposal safely. Zero judgment — we just need to route it correctly.

The chatbot handles most questions in under a minute. For anything it can't resolve, it routes to a human — typically same-day response on weekdays.

Chatbot: Always available.  Human response: Mon–Fri, 8am–6pm. Weekend contacts are reviewed Monday morning.
